App Overview & Research Methodology

Zomato is one of India's largest food-tech platforms, operating in 789 cities and serving over 100 million monthly active users. Founded in 2008 as a restaurant discovery platform, it has since evolved into a full-stack food delivery ecosystem offering instant ordering, live order tracking, restaurant discovery, and user-generated reviews.

For this case study, I conducted a heuristic evaluation of the Zomato Android app (v17.x) using Nielsen's 10 Usability Heuristics as the evaluation framework. I analyzed three primary user flows: discovery → ordering → tracking. Research methods used include: competitive analysis (vs Swiggy, Uber Eats), five simulated user scenario walkthroughs, and screen-by-screen friction logging.

Strengths & Weaknesses Analysis

This section evaluates Zomato against core UX heuristics. The analysis is grounded in observed interaction patterns and compared with competitor benchmarks (Swiggy, Uber Eats).

✓ Strengths

  • Rich restaurant discovery with high-quality food photography that drives appetite appeal and click-through
  • Live GPS order tracking with real-time rider location — significantly reduces post-order anxiety
  • Gold/Pro loyalty program with tangible discounts creates strong retention and repeat purchase behavior
  • Comprehensive review system with photos, ratings, and filters (recency, rating, dish-specific) builds social proof
  • Smart reorder feature surfaces previously ordered items immediately — reduces cognitive load for habitual users
  • Schedule order feature (order in advance) is rare among competitors and adds unique utility
  • Clean information hierarchy on restaurant cards: name, rating, cuisine, ETA, and cost all visible before tapping

✗ Weaknesses

  • Hidden delivery costs revealed only at checkout violates "no surprises" principle and increases cart abandonment
  • Address confirmation modal on every app launch is repetitive friction — violates "efficiency of use" heuristic
  • Excessive promotional content (banners, pop-ups, tooltips) competes with functional content, overwhelming users
  • Onboarding lacks personalization — new users receive generic recommendations with no preference capture
  • Complaint/refund flow is complex (5+ screens with repetitive confirmations) — creates friction at highest-stress moments
  • No "dark mode" support — jarring in low-light environments; a basic accessibility gap
  • Rider contact requires 3+ taps — during time-sensitive delivery issues, this creates unnecessary friction

Overall, Zomato excels at the pre-order discovery phase but struggles at the critical checkout and post-order stages, where trust and transparency are most important. The platform's heavy monetization of UI space (ads, upsells, banners) has gradually degraded the clean experience it was known for in earlier versions.

3 Proposed UX Improvements

Each improvement is grounded in a specific usability issue identified during the journey mapping and heuristic evaluation. Mockup sketches and expected impact metrics are included.

1
Transparent Pricing Indicator (Pre-Cart)
Problem

Delivery fees, surge charges, and platform fees are revealed only on the final checkout screen. Users who see a ₹10 delivery fee on the restaurant card often discover a ₹49–89 actual fee at checkout — creating a "bait-and-switch" perception that drives cart abandonment. Studies show unexpected shipping/delivery costs are the #1 cause of e-commerce cart abandonment globally.

Proposed Solution

Add a real-time "delivery fee estimate" badge directly on restaurant cards and at the top of the menu screen, updating dynamically based on the user's confirmed address. If surge pricing is active, show a clear indicator with explanation (e.g., "Rain surge active – fee may be higher"). This implements the "visibility of system status" heuristic and eliminates checkout surprise.

2
Personalized Onboarding Flow with Preference Capture
Problem

New Zomato users are immediately dropped into a generic discovery feed with no consideration for dietary preferences, cuisine tastes, or budget range. This forces users to apply manual filters from the very first session, increasing cognitive load and reducing the perceived intelligence of the platform. Competitor apps like Swiggy Instamart use simple onboarding questions to immediately personalize the feed.

Proposed Solution

Introduce a 3-screen onboarding flow (post sign-up, pre-home) that captures: (1) dietary preference — vegetarian, non-vegetarian, vegan; (2) favorite cuisines — multi-select from 8 options; (3) typical budget per meal — low/medium/high. This data immediately personalizes the home feed, restaurant ranking, and "For You" recommendations section. Each preference screen should take under 10 seconds to complete. Add a "Skip for now" option to respect user autonomy.

3
One-Tap Rider Contact on Tracking Screen
Problem

When users need to contact their delivery rider (e.g., to direct them to a gate, report being unavailable briefly, or inquire about a delay), the current flow requires: tapping the tracking screen → finding the "Help" option → selecting "Contact rider" → choosing call or chat. This 3–4 tap sequence is frustrating precisely when users are most anxious — when their food is late or there's a delivery difficulty. Heuristic violated: "Flexibility and efficiency of use."

Proposed Solution

Surface a persistent "Contact Rider" action bar on the active order tracking screen with two directly tappable buttons: a phone icon (masked call via platform) and a chat icon (in-app messaging). These should be visible without scrolling and sized to thumb-friendly tap targets (min 44×44px). Additionally, add a quick-message template system (e.g., "I'm at the gate", "Please wait 5 minutes") to enable contactless communication without typing.

Conclusion & Key Takeaways

Zomato is a well-engineered food discovery and delivery platform that has earned its market leadership through strong restaurant partnerships, high-quality food imagery, and a reliable tracking experience. However, the platform shows signs of UX debt accumulated through rapid feature addition and heavy commercialization of the interface.

The three improvements proposed in this case study address the most critical friction points across the user journey. Collectively, they focus on a single theme: radical transparency and reducing effort at moments of high user stress — checkout, onboarding, and mid-delivery.

Core UX Principle Applied Throughout This Study

"Don't make me think" — Steve Krug. Every decision in this analysis is tested against this principle. A good food delivery app should feel effortless. The moment a user has to figure something out — whether it's a hidden fee, a buried filter, or a contact button — trust erodes. The improvements proposed here reduce cognitive load at the highest-stakes moments in the user journey, which is where loyalty is truly won or lost.

The UX improvements suggested are practical, technically feasible, and each addresses a specific, measurable heuristic violation. Implementation of all three could realistically lead to measurable improvements in order completion rates, Day-1 retention, and post-delivery satisfaction scores — making them high-ROI investments for Zomato's product team.
